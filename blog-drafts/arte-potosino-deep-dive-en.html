1. Pre-Hispanic Art: The Huastecs and Their Master Sculptors

Before the Spanish conquest, Potosino territory was home to civilizations that left an extraordinary artistic legacy

The region we know today as San Luis Potos√≠ was inhabited by various indigenous cultures before the arrival of the Spanish. According to the National Institute of Indigenous Peoples (INPI), among the most important groups were the Huastecs (Teenek), the Chichimecs and the Pames (Xi'oi).

Of all these cultures, it was the Huastecs who developed the most impressive sculptural tradition. Unlike other Mesoamerican cultures, the Huastecs specialized in carving sandstone, creating slender and detailed sculptures that are now considered masterpieces of Mexican pre-Hispanic art.

üìö

Characteristics of Huastec Art

"Sculpture is the most impressive means of expression of the Huastecs since their works are very characteristic, with very different features from other Mesoamerican cultures. Most sculptures are made with sandstone, a sedimentary rock composed of quartz sand."

‚Äî Pueblos Originarios

Techniques and Materials

Huastec artists crafted excellent pieces using various materials: sandstone, gold, copper, jade, onyx, obsidian and ceramics. They also produced amate paper (fig bark) and developed a distinctive tradition of body painting and scarification that was immortalized in their sculptures.

The Sierra de Tanchipa, located in the Huasteca Potosina, served as a natural quarry for sandstone. This raw material was used by artists to carve sculptures of rulers, priestesses and various public stelae that showed events relevant to the community.

Huastec Painting

Huastec painting is known mainly through the ceramics they made. Pots and vessels show synthetic and abstract designs outlined in black or red on clay. There is also evidence of mural painting, such as the Taqu√≠n mural in San Luis Potos√≠, which represents one of the few surviving pictorial samples from this culture.

Tamtoc (also known as Tamohi) is an archaeological site located in the municipality of Tamu√≠n, San Luis Potos√≠. Three main periods of occupation have been identified: the oldest occurred around 600 BC, corresponding to the city's emergence. The second occupation took place between 600 and 900 AD, distinguished by the arrival of a cultural current that redesigned the urban appearance. The last period occurred between 900 and 1350 AD, characterized by significant population growth.

The Huastec Adolescent: Masterpiece of Pre-Hispanic Art

Without a doubt, the most famous piece found at Tamtoc is the Huastec Adolescent (also known as the Adolescent of Tamu√≠n). According to the National Museum of Anthropology, this sculpture was carved in sandstone and measures 145 cm in height.

"The sculpture represents a young male individual, standing, naked, with cranial deformation, filed upper teeth and large holes in the earlobes to wear ear ornaments. His nudity qualifies him as a deity, a characteristic feature of Huastec deities."

National Museum of Anthropology

Technical file of the piece

The sculpture was discovered in 1917 by Walter Staub in the western structure of the archaeological zone. On his back he carries a child hanging from a band, which according to researchers represents the sun. The most characteristic features are the carved designs on the body, which represent tattoos or body paint with symbols of corn cobs, green stone beads, the water serpent and signs related to the cult of Eh√©catl-Quetzalc√≥atl.

The Venus of Tamtoc (Scarified Woman)

Another monumental piece is the Venus of Tamtoc or Scarified Woman. This fragmented female sculpture dates approximately from 600 BC, making it one of the oldest sculptures in Mesoamerica at about 2,500 years old. It measures approximately 50 cm wide by just over one meter tall, and was discovered in 2005 during the rescue work of Monument 32.

It is decorated with small protuberances or scarifications that form lines and rhombuses. According to archaeologists, these scarifications allude to calendar days, solar cycles and lunar cycles. In 2021, both the Scarified Woman and the Huastec Adolescent were exhibited at the Quai Branly-Jacques Chirac Museum in Paris, along with 300 other Mexican pre-Hispanic pieces.

3. Textile Art: The Rebozos of Santa Mar√≠a del R√≠o

A tradition dating from pre-Hispanic times that has won national and international awards

On the entrance arch to Santa Mar√≠a del R√≠o, on the federal highway, it reads: "Santa Mar√≠a del R√≠o, birthplace of the rebozo". And it's no exaggeration. According to the Ministry of Culture, rebozo-making in this municipality dates from pre-Hispanic times, and today it produces the finest and most recognized rebozos in Mexico.

What makes these rebozos unique is not just their beauty, but the extraordinary manufacturing process. Each piece is made by two people: the first makes the weaving (winding, warping, picking, tying, dyeing and weaving the fabric); the second does the fringing. This entire process takes approximately one year.

The Process of Creating a Rebozo

1
Winding and Warping

The threads of synthetic silk, cotton or artisela are prepared, organizing them on the backstrap loom.

2
Picking and Tying

Ikat technique: threads are tied and grouped to create patterns before dyeing.

3
Dyeing

Traditionally with natural dyes: fermented indigo for blue, cochineal for carmine, and "barbilla" (lichen) for browns.

4
Weaving

On backstrap loom, with techniques like bolitas, lloviznas, grecas, caramelos, peinecillos, open dyes, arrows and hearts.

5
Fringing

A second artisan does the fringing: elaborate fringes that can be so fine that the rebozo passes through a ring.

The Seven Traditional Designs

There are seven traditional designs based on ancient patterns:

  • Design 1 - Bolita: Characteristic circular pattern
  • Design 2 - Caramelo: Has seven different colors
  • Design 3 - S Grecas: Undulating geometric pattern
  • Design 4 - Pinto Abierto: More spaced design
  • Design 5 - Swords or Arrows: Pointed motifs
  • Design 6 - Facing Birds: Stylized birds facing each other
  • Design 10 - Wide Greca: Wide geometric pattern

üìå The "Barbilla" or "Scented" Rebozo: This name comes from the lichen called "barbilla" that grows in the ravines of the region, traditionally used to dye various shades of brown. Rebozos dyed with this material retain a characteristic scent.

This garment is a story told by women. Stitch by stitch, the Teenek universe is captured. Each thread symbolizes elements such as protection, love and nature. From childhood, Teenek women learn to embroider the garments they will use throughout their lives.

The Meaning of Colors

The Petob: The Traditional Headdress

Complementing the dhayemlaab, Teenek women wear the petob, a headdress made of a large braid formed by colored yarn intertwined with the woman's hair. The colors of the petob communicate marital status: if single she will wear only green and pink; when married, red and orange yarns are added; if widowed, she can wear any colors she wishes.

For the Wix√°rikas, it was in Wirikuta where "creation" emerged. Cerro El Quemado in Real de Catorce represents the eastern cardinal point in their sacred geography. Here grows peyote (hikuri), a sacred cactus they consume in rituals to communicate with the gods and receive the "gift of seeing."

Huichol beadwork art is recognized worldwide for its extraordinary precision and symbolism. The tsikuri, known as "god's eye," is a colorful creation consisting of one or more yarn rhombuses spun on reed crosses. They serve as protection and are made particularly to safeguard children's health. For each year of life, the father spins a rhombus, up to five ‚Äî the sacred Wix√°rika number.
